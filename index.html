About

Exploring the Universe through its large-scale structure

I am a cosmologist studying the distribution of matter across the Universe and developing statistical tools to extract the information encoded in galaxy surveys.

I completed my PhD in Astrophysics at Queen Mary University of London and am currently a postdoctoral researcher at Ludwig-Maximilians-Universität München.

Research

Relativistic effects in cosmological observables

I investigate how velocities, projection effects and relativistic corrections influence cosmological observables such as the power spectrum, bispectrum and trispectrum.

01

Relativistic effects in large-scale structure

Galaxy surveys observe light arriving along the past lightcone. This means that the observed distribution of galaxies includes velocity, lensing, gravitational and projection effects in addition to the underlying density.

My work studies how these contributions modify the shapes and symmetries of cosmological observables.

02

Higher-order statistics

The bispectrum and trispectrum contain information that cannot be captured by the two-point function alone.

I study relativistic contributions to these higher-order correlations, including odd-parity and apparently parity-violating signatures generated by observational effects.

03

Symmetry, parity and cosmological geometry

Symmetry provides a powerful way to separate physical signals from observational effects.

I investigate how line-of-sight geometry, wide-angle effects and relativistic corrections generate signatures that change under parity transformations.
